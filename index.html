Portfolio Website

I loved designing my portfolio because I could choose elements based on my likes and dislikes. Through my portfolio, I am sharing not only my accomplishments and experiences, but also who I am, which added an extra challenge to the project.

Using a single page design keeps the portfolio clean, while features within the page give options for additional content. Carousels and modal windows allow visitors to choose what projects or experiences they want to read more about.

My design process always starts with sketches on physical paper to figure out the organization of a site and the rough design. For my site, I sketched a desktop design and a mobile design to nail down the general look and feel I wanted to create before I started coding.

To build my site, I decided to use Bootstrap, which meant I had to learn Bootstrap. Bootstrap's tools allowed me to build the basic structure of my portfolio, and from there I was able to add my own style and design to make the site feel like mine.

Over the course of building out the site, I came across a challenge I did not expect. While it was nice to not schedule group meetings or consult other members before making big changes, I missed brainstorming and problem solving with a team.

Through my time working on my site, I gained a greater appreciation for the differences between working alone and working with a team. There are advantages and challenges to every working situation, and I used those differences to create an end product I was proud to share.

Smart Mirror

The Smart Mirror was built in my User Interface course senior year. My teammate and I designed a smart mirror to be displayed in the College Computing and Technology at Lipscomb University. It uses the OpenJarvis AI for voice recognition to navigate through the pages. We included local weather, a calendar of events, local traffic, and a tutorial page for people using the mirror for the first time.

We used the MagicMirror codebase to get started. My teammate worked mostly with the hardware and the voice recognition code, while I focused more on the design and content we wanted to share on the mirror. We brainstormed which modules would be helpful to current students and prospective students, as we wanted the mirror to be useful. Because we completed the mirror for our user interface course, the user’s interaction with the design was at the forefront of our minds when we first started sketching.

Originally, we wanted to use hand gestures to navigate through the mirror, but due to hardware limitations, we had to pivot. We decided to use voice recognition because it is still intuitive for the user and does not require any expensive technology. To wake Jarvis up, users say “Jarvis.” The hotword that lets Jarvis know the user is saying a command is “please.” Some example commands are “Jarvis, please show the forecast,” which shows the weather, or “Jarvis, please show the traffic,” which shows the local traffic.

CodeMentor

CodeMentor is a site developed by a team of three students for my senior seminar course as an educational resource for college students in computing disciplines. The prototype consolidates resources for specific college courses at specific universities so students can learn the material ahead of time for the courses in their future or review topics after they take the course. Students log in to their account, and their forward progress is saved. They can also take notes that are saved within the site and correspond to specific topics and resource links.

We used Django as our web framework for the project and a Bootstrap template for the basic structure of the front end of the site. My two team members worked on the back-end logic, and I focused on the front-end development and project management.

I drew sketches of the site based on user needs, and then we found a template that had the same basic structure of my original design. In addition to creating the front end of the site, I aided in designing the database schema design and problem solving for some of the back-end problems.
